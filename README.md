## The Nature of This Edition

This volume presents the New Testament in a format designed specifically for the reader. By removing verse numbers and traditional formatting markers, this Reader’s Edition allows the narrative and apostolic letters to flow as continuous accounts. The text utilizes the **American Standard Version (1901)** and retains the formal language of that era, including the use of *thee*, *thou*, and *ye*. This choice preserves the grammatical distinction between the singular and plural second person, providing a level of precision often lost in modern translations.

## The Source of the Text

The English base for this project is the work of **Robert Adam Boyd**, who adjusted the 1901 American Standard Version to align with the **Byzantine Priority** manuscript tradition. This ensures that the English text reflects the consensus of the historic Greek-speaking Church.

To facilitate a fluid reading experience, the italics traditionally used for supplied words have been removed, and the text has been rendered in a natural paragraph layout. Selected other archaic or otherwise rarely used English words have been updated to modern equivalents.

## The Case for Byzantine Priority

The following summary of the Byzantine Priority position is drawn from the work of Robert Adam Boyd. In his own research and preface, Boyd highlights an observation made by **Dr. Maurice Robinson** in the essay, *The Case for Byzantine Priority*:

> "Modern eclecticism creates a text which, within repeated short sequences, rapidly degenerates into one possessing no support among manuscript, versional, or patristic witnesses."

Boyd notes that this problem deteriorates further as the scope of sequential variation increases. In other words, when the text-critical decisions of modern editions are considered over the course of a few verses, it is often the case that the resulting text as a whole has no support in any Greek manuscript, ancient translation, or quotation from the church fathers; rather, it is a **conjectural text**. 

This critical flaw of the modern eclectic approach has never been adequately addressed by its proponents. As a result, many prefer the Byzantine text, which is based on the overwhelming majority of Greek manuscripts.
